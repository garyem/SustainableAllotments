Deep Bed Farming: Sustainability Guide

This guide explains how deep bed farming can affect the environment and how you can reduce any problems. The information is explained in a straightforward way to help all growers, no matter their experience.

Sustainability Issue Why It's a Problem How to Fix or Reduce It
Buying Soil or Compost Bringing in lots of soil or compost from far away uses fuel and may include non-sustainable materials like peat. - Make compost using your own kitchen and garden waste.
- Use green manures to build healthy soil.
- Buy compost that is local and peat-free.
Materials for Raised Beds Wood that is treated with chemicals can harm soil. Plastic can break down and pollute. Concrete is energy-intensive to make. - Use natural, untreated wood like larch or cedar.
- Reuse bricks or other natural materials.
- Avoid plastic and pressure-treated wood.
Digging Too Deep Digging too much can damage helpful microbes and insects in the soil. It can also release carbon into the air. - Use a "no-dig" method and add compost on top of the soil.
- Let worms and roots improve the soil naturally over time.
Watering in Dry Weather Raised beds can dry out fast in hot or windy weather, which wastes water. - Cover the soil with mulch like straw or compost.
- Collect rainwater in water butts.
- Use drip irrigation to save water.
Too Much Fertiliser Adding too much compost or manure can cause too many nutrients, which harms soil and nearby water. - Check how your plants grow before adding more compost.
- Add compost slowly over time.
- Use a mix of materials, not just manure.
